the comparison people usually make is wrong. nobody serious thinks dsa is about to install a politburo or enforce religious law. that's not the point. the point is about *process* — how a small, disciplined faction takes over a much bigger coalition that was never really organized around the same goals.

## the pattern

in soviet russia and revolutionary iran, the group that ended up in charge wasn't the majority of the revolution. it was the most organized faction within a broad, messy coalition of people who mostly just wanted the old regime gone. bolsheviks operated inside a much larger anti-tsarist movement. khomeini's islamists were one current among many — liberals, leftists, nationalists — who all wanted the shah out. in both cases, the disciplined minority let the broader coalition do the heavy lifting, then set the agenda once the old order collapsed.

the moderates in these coalitions weren't stupid. they just weren't as committed to a specific ideological project. they had day jobs. they wanted reform, not revolution. and that asymmetry — between people who want *something* to change and people who want a *specific thing* to change — is what makes capture possible.

## the dsa playbook

dsa isn't building a third party. they're running in democratic primaries. they're embedding in local party organizations, unions, activist networks, and safe-blue districts where a primary challenge from the left is the only real electoral threat. they don't need to win over republicans or swing voters. they just need to be more organized than the other democrats in the room.

this is strategic, not accidental. forming a third party in the american system is a losing move — you split the vote and elect your opponent. but running inside the democratic party lets you use the entire coalition's infrastructure, voter base, and brand while pushing a platform most of that coalition didn't sign up for.

most democratic voters are there because of abortion rights, healthcare, opposing republicans, or some mix of practical concerns. they're not there because they read the dsa platform on worker ownership of the means of production. but they don't have to be. the coalition holds together because the alternative — republicans — is unacceptable. that gives dsa room to operate.

## why it works

the mechanics are simple. most people don't vote in primaries. most people don't show up to party meetings. most people don't volunteer, canvass, or organize. dsa members do. when turnout is low and engagement is thin, a motivated minority punches way above its weight.

incumbents in safe-blue seats used to coast. now they have to worry about primary challenges from the left, which means they either move left preemptively or risk getting replaced by someone who will. the overton window shifts not because the median voter changed their mind, but because the people who actually show up changed.

this is how you get a democratic party where positions that were fringe a decade ago — abolish ice, defund the police, federal jobs guarantee — get treated as legitimate policy options. not because 60% of americans support them, but because the activists who set the tone of the conversation do.

## the concern

the worry isn't revolution. it's ideological capture. a situation where the party's direction is increasingly set by its most committed faction, not its median voter. where elected officials respond more to activist pressure than to the broader electorate because activists are the ones who determine primaries.

this doesn't require conspiracy or bad faith. it's just what happens when one group inside a coalition is more organized, more ideologically coherent, and more willing to challenge incumbents than everyone else. the moderates aren't being purged. they're being outworked.

the historical parallel isn't about outcomes — nobody's predicting gulags or theocracy. it's about the process by which a minority faction, operating inside a larger coalition, gradually redefines what that coalition stands for. that process has happened before. the question is whether it's happening now.
